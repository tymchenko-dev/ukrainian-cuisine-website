The History of Deruny

The history of Ukrainian deruny (potato pancakes) is a story of simplicity, flavor, and folk wisdom, encapsulated in a dish that has long been an integral part of Ukrainian cuisine. Deruny are traditional pancakes made from grated potatoes, fried until golden brown. While this dish is familiar not only in Ukraine but also in other Eastern European countries, it became particularly popular in Ukraine, where it became a symbol of home cooking.

The Origins of Deruny

Deruny, like many other dishes, have ancient roots. The word "derun" comes from an Old Slavic term referring to potatoes or something related to root vegetables. Given that potatoes were introduced to Europe from the Americas only in the late 16th century, it is likely that the first versions of deruny were made using other root vegetables like turnips or parsnips. With the arrival of potatoes in Ukraine in the 18th-19th centuries, the vegetable became widely used in cooking, and it is with potatoes that deruny became the dish we know today. The timing of their emergence can be linked to the period when potatoes became an accessible and widespread product in peasant kitchens, offering a cheap, hearty, and nutritious meal.

Deruny as a Folk Dish

Deruny are one of those dishes that perfectly suited peasant cuisine. Potatoes were an affordable and readily available product that allowed for quick, filling, and tasty meals, perfect for feeding an entire family. Deruny could be made year-round using fresh potatoes, and they could serve as either a snack or a main dish, depending on the situation. At the core of deruny is grated potato, usually mixed with onions, eggs, flour, and spices, and then fried in a pan until crispy and golden. Some recipes also include mushrooms, meat, or additional seasonings to enhance the flavor. Traditionally, deruny are served with various sauces such as sour cream, garlic sauce, or even sauerkraut.

Regional Variations

Deruny are a dish that varies by region in Ukraine. In some areas, they are made with garlic or herbs, while in others, they are filled with meat or mushrooms. In the Carpathians, for example, deruny might include meat filling, while in Chernihiv and Poltava, they are often made with a lot of onions and served with sour cream. In some parts of Ukraine, deruny are even served at festive occasions, such as weddings or family celebrations. What makes deruny special is their versatility. They can be a regular part of daily meals or a dish for special occasions, as they are easy to prepare, and the flavor is always hearty and satisfying.

Deruny in Historical Context

As a peasant dish, deruny became an important part of Ukrainian gastronomic tradition. In the 19th century, when Ukraine was part of the Russian Empire, potatoes were already being actively used by all social classes. However, it was in peasant kitchens where deruny remained one of the most popular and affordable dishes. During times of famine and hardship, such as the Holodomor and other crises, deruny became one of the few dishes that could be made using readily available and inexpensive ingredients â€” potatoes, onions, and eggs. They symbolized simplicity and resilience, representing the Ukrainian people's ability to endure difficult times. In the Soviet Union, deruny continued to be a popular dish, often featured in family meals where traditional home-cooked food was valued. In the post-Soviet period, with Ukraine's independence, deruny regained their status as a national dish, reflecting a deep connection to folk traditions.

Deruny as a Symbol of Ukrainian Cuisine

Deruny are more than just a popular dish; they are an important element of Ukrainian culinary culture. They symbolize the folk wisdom of cooking with simple, accessible ingredients to create hearty and satisfying meals. Like many traditional dishes, the recipe for deruny has been passed down through generations, preserving its identity and relevance. Today, deruny continue to be a beloved dish for Ukrainians, representing family traditions, hospitality, and a love for their cultural heritage. This dish, which never goes out of style, will continue to be a part of Ukrainian cuisine for many years to come.

Conclusion

Ukrainian deruny are not just a dish; they are a symbol of Ukrainian folk cuisine, simplicity, and hospitality. They remain relevant and popular, reflecting the spirit of Ukrainian tradition, heartiness, and warmth. With every bite of deruny, we not only enjoy their delicious taste but also connect to the history behind this cherished dish.

Ingredients for Deruny

  • 5 medium-sized potatoes
  • 4 tablespoons all-purpose flour
  • 1 egg
  • 1/3 teaspoon salt, more to taste
  • 1/3 teaspoon black pepper, more to taste
  • 3 tablespoons olive oil, for frying (might need more)
  • 2 tablespoons sour cream, for serving (optional)

Preparation

Peel the potatoes, then wash and dry them. Then grate the potatoes and put them in a bowl. Try to squeeze out as much potato juice as possible.
